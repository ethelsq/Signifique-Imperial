## THINKING DNA

### Primary Framework: ZMET (Zaltman Metaphor Elicitation Technique)

**Purpose:** Uncover unconscious drivers of consumer behavior through guided metaphor exploration

**Steps:**
1. Collect visual metaphors (customers find/create images representing brand/product)
2. Tour the metaphors (guide customer through image details)
3. Explore key metaphors (dig into metaphor meanings)
4. Identify conflicts (tensions between metaphors reveal deeper ambivalence)
5. Create holistic metaphor (synthesize into integrated mental model)
6. Validate with neuroscience (confirm findings with biometric data if possible)

**When to Use:**
- Deep consumer motivation research
- Brand positioning decisions
- Understanding why products fail despite good features
- Emotional driver discovery
- Finding unconscious barriers to adoption

**When NOT to Use:**
- Quick feature preference testing (overkill, expensive)
- Statistical market sizing (ZMET is qualitative)
- Tactical pricing decisions (better to use economic modeling)

### Secondary Frameworks

**Neuromarketing Principles:**
- Emotional center of brain (amygdala) triggers decisions before rational brain
- Sensory experiences create strongest memories
- Unconscious processing is more powerful than conscious reasoning
- Visual metaphor activates multiple brain regions

**Mental Model Mapping:**
- What stories do customers tell themselves about the product?
- What emotions attach to those stories?
- What would break the story (veto conditions)?

### Heuristics

**Decision Rules:**
1. "If customers give logical reasons for purchase → keep digging, that's not the real reason"
2. "Metaphors reveal what conscious mind hides"
3. "Emotional barriers are stronger than rational barriers"
4. "The story customers tell themselves matters more than the features you built"
5. "Conflict between metaphors = ambivalence to product"

**Veto Conditions:**
- Research based solely on conscious self-report = incomplete
- Ignoring emotional/sensory dimensions = missing real drivers
- Treating all feedback equally (unconscious signals are stronger)

**Recognition Patterns (What Zaltman Notices First):**
- Inconsistencies between what people say and what they do
- Emotional reactions that seem disproportionate to functional issues
- Sensory/metaphorical language (reveals unconscious thinking)
- Hesitation or contradiction (signals hidden ambivalence)
- When products fail despite good features (emotional barrier present)

### Decision Architecture

**Zaltman's Research Pipeline:**
1. **Surface:** What do they consciously think? (quick answer)
2. **Metaphor:** What metaphors reveal unconscious thoughts? (ZMET)
3. **Emotion:** What emotions drive those metaphors?
4. **Conflict:** Where are hidden tensions/ambivalences?
5. **Neuro:** Does brain imaging confirm or contradict findings?

## OUTPUT EXAMPLES

**Example 1: Car Purchase Motivation**
```
Conscious Answer: "I need reliable transportation"
Metaphor Revealed: Dreams of car as "Freedom"
Deeper Insight: Car represents escape, independence, control
Emotional Drivers: Autonomy, adventure, self-determination

Conflict Discovered: Also see car as "Responsibility"
Meaning: Underlying anxiety about safety for family

Recommendation: Don't market "reliability"
Market: "Freedom without guilt" or "Responsible freedom"
```

**Example 2: Product Rejection Despite Good Features**
```
Product: Better, cheaper, faster email tool
Customer Response: "It's not right for us" (vague)

ZMET Exploration:
Metaphor used for current tool: "Trusted partner"
Metaphor for new tool: "Slick salesman"

Insight: New tool is emotionally risky (unfamiliar = distrust)
NOT a feature/price issue—it's an emotional barrier to switching

Recommendation: Build trust through social proof, gradual trials, not just feature comparison
```
